Hong Kong

Alfred Eriksson, Hampus Berglund

The island of Hong kong became occupied by the british on January 25 1841. the british used Hong kong as a military staging point, a place where equipment and troops is assembled before going into war. China was defeated and was forced to give up the island.


Culture


Majority of Hong Kong is Cantonese which makes Cantonese culture the most common culture. But there's not only Cantonese people living in Hong Kong. Shanghainese, British, Indians and Jews are also a big part of Hong Kong's population. This makes Hong Kong's culture very diverse and interesting. The food all over Hong Kong is really good. Colonisation brought a lot of western type of cooking To Hong Kong. This western style is being combined with the chinese cooking. It’s Chinese cuisine together with western cuisine. Hong kong is very special when it comes to languages. Chinese and English is the official languages but the actual languages that are being spoken are chinese english mandarin and cantonese. Hong Kong has a lot of religions. Taoism is very common in Hong Kong and its very special. fung shui is believed by many people in Hong Kong. fung shui means “wind and water” it teaches humans how to achieve harmony by the forces of nature and change. something interesting about taoism is also feng shui which is how buildings are supposed to be positioned that agrees with spiritual forces. When you are in Hong Kong you are going to notice the big holes in the buildings. These holes are called dragon gates and are build because dragons are believed to be bringers of good fortune. blocking the dragon's path could bring misfortune and that's why almost all buildings have these weird scapes and holes.


Tradtion


Hong kong is very similar great britain as it used to be a colony and similar to china due to its geographic location. Many of the hongkongese traditions are mainly centered around the lunar year and there are many annual festivals. One of the biggest is the celebration of the new lunar year or “Chinese new year”. As the name may reveal this traditional festival is is celebrate the new year. Another traditional celebration is the spring lantern festival. It gets celebrated on the fifteenth day of the new lunar year. Alongside the spring lantern festival there is also the “seven sisters festival” which is commonly referred as the chinese version of valentines day. Many couple celebrate by taking walks in lantern lit parks. There are alot of festivals in Hong Kong during the year. Another example of this is Cheung Chau Bun Festival which is held in a small fishing village. This festival is held in honor of the god pak tai. This festival is held on the fourth to ninth day of the 4th lunar month. This celebration commonly involves baking buns and dressing up in costumes.
